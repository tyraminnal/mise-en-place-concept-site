№ 01
Chef

A voice that asks the right question, instead of telling you what to do.

Recipe apps treat every cook like a beginner. Chef adapts. If you already know how to temper mustard seeds, it gets out of your way. If you don't, it asks what you're seeing — and listens.

Socratic dialogue

Asks first, instructs after.

Chef teaches by drawing on your senses, not overriding them. Every step ends with a question, not a deadline.

Skill calibration

Skip what you know.

Tell Chef once that you grew up watching your nani make masala. It won't explain caramelized onions to you for the rest of your life.

Hands-free

Your hands stay in the dough.

No tapping, no swiping, no accidentally smearing turmeric on the screen. Voice in, voice out.

№ 03
Rescue Mode

Triggered the moment something starts going wrong.

Acoustic Intelligence picks up the sound of oil getting too hot, a pan scorching, a pot boiling over. Rescue Mode interrupts the recipe and walks you out — without judgment.

Rescue mode · activated 0.4s ago

Your onions are darkening fast. Pull the pan off the heat.

It's okay — this is salvageable. Three quick moves and we resume the recipe from where we left off. No starting over.

01

Move the pan.

Off the burner, onto a cool surface. Don't add water yet.

02

Add a splash.

One tablespoon of water. The hiss will stop the cooking instantly.

03

Resume from step 04.

You're back. Heat low this time. Chef will warn you sooner next round.

№ 04
Burn Book

A journal of what went wrong, kept in your own voice.

Private, unsearchable, never used for training. The place where your cooking memory actually lives — failures, fixes, and the adjustments you'll make instinctively next time.

Tuesday, 12 MarchAchaar

Mustard seeds before oil.

Tried mom's mango pickle. Did the seeds first then poured hot oil over — they didn't crackle, they just sat there looking sad.

Next time: oil hot first. Then seeds. Then everything else.
Friday, 15 MarchPasta

"Al dente" is not a feeling.

I keep overcooking pasta because I taste it once, panic it's hard, and leave it two more minutes. Two more minutes is always too long.

Set a 9-minute timer. Don't taste. Trust the box.
Sunday, 24 MarchDaal

Tadka should sound like rain.

Chef said curry leaves hitting oil should sound like "the first three seconds of rain." Mine were silent. Oil wasn't hot enough.

Wait for the shimmer. Then count to ten. Then add.
Wednesday, 03 AprilBread

The dough wasn't proofing because the kitchen was 64°F.

Three hours, no rise. I assumed the yeast was dead. It wasn't — my apartment was.

Cold kitchen → oven lowest setting 60 seconds, off, proof inside.

№ 06
Acoustic Intelligence

The kitchen has a vocabulary. Mise speaks it.

Cooking is full of sounds that mean something specific — the rolling boil, the sizzle of a properly heated pan, the silence of a flame that just went out.

Listening, not recording.

Audio is processed on-device and discarded the moment a sound is classified. No transcript. No audio saved to a server. No model training on what your kitchen sounds like.

Designed to be deleted applies to your data too. Every session ends, and the data ends with it.

Rolling boil
Continuous low rumble · safe to walk away
Hard sizzle
Sharp crackle, rising · oil too hot · turn down
Wet hiss
Moisture hitting hot pan · good sear starting
Silent simmer
No movement on surface · flame likely out
Pop & crackle
Mustard or cumin seeds · tadka ready · add next
