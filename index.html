№ 01
Chef

A voice that asks the right question, instead of telling you what to do.

Recipe apps treat every cook like a beginner. Chef adapts. If you already know how to temper mustard seeds, it gets out of your way. If you don't, it asks what you're seeing and listens.

Socratic dialogue

Asks first, instructs after.

Chef teaches by drawing on your senses, not overriding them. Every step ends with a question, not a deadline.

Skill calibration

Skip what you know.

Tell Chef once that you grew up watching your nani make curry. It won't explain blending sautéed vegetables to you for the rest of your life.

Hands-free

Your hands stay in the dough.

No tapping, no swiping, no accidentally smearing flour on the screen. Voice in, voice out.

№ 02
Rescue Mode

Triggered the moment something starts going wrong.

Acoustic Intelligence picks up the sound of oil getting too hot, a pan scorching, a pot boiling over. Rescue Mode interrupts the recipe and walks you out of the danger zone, without judgment.

Rescue mode · activated 0.4s ago

Your onions are darkening fast. Pull the pan off the heat.

It's okay, this is salvageable. Three quick moves and we resume the recipe from where we left off. No starting over.

01

Move the pan.

Off the burner, onto a cool surface. Don't add water yet.

02

Add a splash.

One tablespoon of water. The hiss will stop the cooking instantly.

03

Resume from step 04.

You're back. Heat low this time. Chef will warn you sooner next round.

№ 03
Burn Book

A journal of what went wrong, kept in your own voice.

The Burn Book is private. It's not a feed, it's not searchable by anyone, and it's not used to train anything. It's the place where your cooking memory actually lives — failures, fixes, the small adjustments your grandmother would have made instinctively.

Tuesday, 12 March Achaar

Mustard seeds before oil.

Tried mom's mango pickle. Did the seeds first then poured hot oil over — they didn't crackle, they just sat there looking sad. Texture went flat. The whole jar tastes like raw mustard.

Next time: oil hot first. Then seeds. Then everything else.
Friday, 15 March Pasta

"Al dente" is not a feeling.

I think I keep overcooking pasta because I taste it once, panic that it's hard, and leave it for two more minutes. Two more minutes is always too long.

Set a 9-minute timer. Don't taste. Trust the box.
Sunday, 24 March Daal

Tadka should sound like rain.

Chef described the sound the curry leaves should make when they hit the oil — "like the first three seconds of rain." Mine were silent. Oil wasn't hot enough. Whole pot tasted like nothing.

Wait for the shimmer. Then count to ten. Then add.
Wednesday, 03 April Bread

The dough wasn't proofing because the kitchen was 64°F.

Three hours, no rise. I assumed the yeast was dead. It wasn't — my apartment was. Chef caught the room temperature when I asked about it on the second hour. Lesson learned.

Cold kitchen → oven on lowest setting for 60 seconds, off, then proof inside.
№ 04
Acoustic Intelligence

The kitchen has a vocabulary. Mise speaks it.

Cooking is full of sounds that mean something specific — the rolling boil, the sizzle of a properly heated pan, the silence of a flame that just went out. Mise listens for them, so you don't have to keep checking.

Listening, not recording.

Audio is processed on-device and discarded the moment a sound is classified. There's no transcript. There's no audio file saved to a server. There's no model training on what your kitchen sounds like.

Designed to be deleted applies to your data too. Every cooking session ends, and the data ends with it.
